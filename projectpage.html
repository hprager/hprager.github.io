Cooper Hewit Design Challenge

by Harry Kayaprager

Posted on March, 12 2017 at 2:55PM

Designing products to help with disabilities

For my design project, I decided to come up with a product to help people with vision impairment or blindness get around. I thought of how dangerous it is to be blind, as you could easily run into something and hurt yourself. I decided to build a sensor array that could warn the user of obstacles, by sensing them and playing a sound to tell the user that there is something ahead of them. I thought that the best place to put it would be on a belt, however that ended up posing some problems.

At first I decided to have only one forward facing sensor, however that wouldn’t be able to protect against anything on the side or behind the person. Also the sensor’s I had have a very limited range, so anything very tall or short would not be detected. For my final design I would have two sensors in the front and the back, that would play different tones, so the user would know which sensor was activated. I also noticed that you would need two of the arrays on either side of you to be fully protected, so the final product would have to be much smaller and less noticeable. Other similar products are very expensive, large, and noticeable and I don’t think that’s necesary. In the final product there would either be more sensors, or the front and back sensors would have a much greater range of detection.

How Sensors Work

Temperature Sensor: This sensor can measure the ambient temperature, however it seems to be somewhat inaccurate and it takes a long time for it to change values. I first used the mini lcd screen to display the actual value, and then I used the buzzer, but since the temperature was constant the buzzer didn’t tell me anything. This type of sensor is used all the time, from making sure your not sick, to knowing your steak is well done. Digital thermometers are really great because they are very accurate and usually change temperatures immediately (this one didn’t for some reason).

Pressure Sensor: This sensor measures the amount of pressure applied to it. You can squeeze it with your fingers to turn on different things. I tried to use the lcd screen again, but it changed values so fast you couldn’t really know how exactly how much pressure you were exerting, also I don’t know what the values represented. I also used the fan but it barely slowed down, and it pretty much was either on or off. Pressure sensors could be used to measure the amount of force needed to do something, like how much force it takes to type on a certain keyboard. Also I think in the new google pixel, you can squeeze the phone to activate the google assistant.

Light Sensor: This sensor measures the amount of light it sees, however I don’t know what value it measures in. When I used the lcd screen the value stayed maxed out at 99 even if I covered the sensor. I also tried the fan, but I couldn’t figure out why It wasn’t changing. Apparently if you shine a flashlight at it the value will change, but I didn’t have a chance to do it. I’m not sure how valuable this sensor could be in the real world, but for any application where something turns on during the day and off during the night, this could be useful. Like with solar panels, you can charge a battery during the day and then automatically start using the battery at night.

Future Tech: VR Immersion

by Harry Kayaprager

Posted on December, 8 2017 at 1:09 PM

Answers to Questions 1-6

For the safari experience I saw the cheetah. It kind of felt like I was there, especially when the cheetah got closer, but VR, especially on phones, is not high enough resolution to seem real. Using the controller helped with the immersion as you could actually select what you are pointing to.

I used the Oculus Rift and the Quill app to try painting in VR. The controls were sortof complicated, but after a couple minutes I figured it all out. It was very similar to the note taking apps on smartphones. The main difference was that you were actually using your hands and the drawing was 3D. I think painting with a drawing tablet or something like that makes more sence because in VR it is really hard to make straight lines and the sortof 3D aspect makes it hard to draw things in consistent distances from the viewer. One advantage could be that the controls were more easily accessible using the touch controllers.

I watched a 360 video on the Gear VR of sailing 49ers which are a type of olympic class sailboat. The video had a lot of views from a helicopter, which was super immersive and kind of disorienting. The video was really cool because you could see the boat as if you were the one sailing it. I think it was very immersive because it seemed like you were actually doing something and moving around rather than most videos where the camera is stationary.

I think 360 photo and video have great potential to be great especially when paired with VR, because a well shot video on a very high resolution can be very immersive. However 360 video technology is lacking in clarity and seeing the details of the scene are impossible. Because of this, it’s not that immersive. 360 video and photography really makes no sense these days unless you own a VR headset because watching things in 360 by clicking and dragging is really tedious.

Google cardboard is the most accessible VR device out of the three. It doesn’t have a controller or even a touchpad, and it is pretty uncomfortable because it’s made of cardboard, has no padding, and doesn’t have a head strap so you have to hold it. Instead of a controller you just look at the thing you want to select and press a button connected to a magnet. The Gear VR is probably the most comfortable because It has just as much padding as the Oculus Rift and it’s lighter. The problem with it is that, without the controller, using the touch pad is super awkward as it has no tactile feedback. Also the controller is hard to use as there is no hand tracking and by moving your hand around normally you could accidentally click on something you didn’t mean to. The oculus has the best all-round experience especially using the touch technology. Having the ability to see and use your hands makes everything so much more immersive, and it allows for many new game ideas. Also the Oculus’ resolution is much better than anything powered by a phone, and the games you can play are much more interesting as it is being powered by a computer.

I think VR technology is a great way to train people in fields such as aviation or medicine. VR could be a cheaper way to train people in fields where doing the real thing can be dangerous. With VR there is no need for a huge expensive simulator like the ones at NASA that astronauts train in. However, VR is still not immersive and cheap enough to make a lot of sense for consumers who just want to play video games.

Technology Immersion Workshop

by Harry Kayaprager

Posted on March 10, 2017 at 9:15 AM

Yesterday in Innovation Lab the class did a technology immersion workshop to learn about the new tech in the lab, and to give us ideas for our projects. We also had to answer questions about the tech and post the answers to our blogs.

The first station that my group encountered was working with the littleBits. The littleBits are magnetically connected electonic modules that can be used to make awesome things, especially when using sensors that read temperature or detect motion. The littleBits are powered by a nine volt battery that is connected to another littleBit in order to power the rest in the chain through the magnets. One of the coolest sensors was the temperature sensor. If you connect it to another littleBit that has a display you can read the temperature. If you connect the power supply to the meter you can read the voltage as well. Another cool sensor was the touch sensor. By connecting that to a led light, you could just touch the sensor to turn it on, however you have to hold it to keep it on. One cool idea I had was to connect the motion sensor to the buzzer to make a motion activated alarm, that is really annoying! The littleBits are a really cool way to create things quickly, easily, and simply using electronics.

The next station was the soldering and solar station. We made solar powered roaches that move (more like buzz) when exposed to the sun. We had to solder the buzzer motor to the solar panel and then hot glue the paperclip legs onto the panel as well. When exposed to the sun the motor vibrates the legs causing the roach to move. The soldering was kindof hard because there were no helping hands to hold the motor in place, and to keep things from moving around. It was cool to use solar power but it did not give off enough power to the motor to really get it to move, so I think a battery would be a more advisable power source. Also you could use the solar panel to charge the battery to have an excess supply of power. Also for fun we glued bobly eyes and a fake wire antenna to the roach too. It was pretty fun especially since I like building things, but since I had soldered before I would have liked something more complicated.

The next station we went to was the virtual reality and 360 media station. First we watched a 360 degree video of swimming with dolphins with the google cardboard. It was pretty cool and definately immersive, but the resolution was not great because 360 video is kindof a new technology. After that we used the 360 degree camera, but sadly it only worked for like five minuites before it's wifi connection cut out. 360 pictures are really cool, but everything is a bit distorted, so it doesnt really capture the real experience compared to normal pictures, but since its immersive it makes up for that. I think it is a great technology for taking panarama shots of nature and landscapes or pretty much any exciting event. Then with the Oculus Rift we played the First Contact game which teaches you how to use Occulus Touch, a hand tracking experience which allows you to see and use your actual hands in the virtual world! There was a big difference between Google Cardboard and the Oculus, especially because the resolution was better and you could actually pick things up and see your hands. Because of that it was much more immersive than the cardboard, making you sometimes forget where you are, constantly switching between the virtual world and the real world. By being able to use your hands it made it feel even more like you are actually there and that you are interacting with your environment, even though the only thing you were actually touching was the controller. By using VR gaming is more immersive and it could be used to train people like pilots how to fly, safer and more immersively than a simulator on a computer. I think VR is an awesome technology, I just hope that AR takes off too.

The next station was the robotics station. The coolest robot was NAO, an extremely advanced and expensive humanoid robot. Sadly, even though it looked cool, it didn't interact with humans as well as I thought it would especially for its price. It took a long time to recognise the commands it could do and it only could understand a couple things. Even when I tried to program it only two functions seemed to work. However when refined more it could become extremely useful as some sort of servant or helper for humans. Another robot was Cosmo. It was kindof like a little tank with a face, which made it very interactive even though it couldn't speak. You could play some cool games with it and control and view it's camera wirelessly. Cosmo could be very helpful to keep kids occupied or if using the tank tracks and wireless contol and camera it could become a military robot to be deployed into dangerous areas for recon. Another robot was a bartending robot, but we did not get it to work. it was kindof like a car with two wheels in the back and two conected together for steering in the front. The last robot was the Kamigami. It was a bug like robot that could be wirelessly controled to run around and it was made mostly out of paper! However one charge only lasted like five minuites and I didn't get to play with it since it was charging. Obviously we have a long way to go with robotics for consumers, but it is promising, with robots like NAO and Cosmo being available.
